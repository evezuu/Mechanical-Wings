# Mechanical Wings Journal

## Project Overview

**Project:** Mechanical Wings  
**Design Time:** Approximately 42 hours  

This journal documents the design process behind the development of my wings. During this, I researching existing mechanisms, developed a functional concept, selected materials, planned the mechanical movement system, and learned about how the wiring works. The goal of the project was to create a pair of wings that could mechanically flare and retract through motorized movement.

Most of the wings I had seen were stationary: they could only move up and down, and they were very limited in motion. However, hers were a lot different. I had to do an indepth study into how linear actuators worked, what pivot mechanisms were, how linkage systems transferred motion, and how different types of materials affected motion. This took me about 5 hours.
 
One of the main challenges I had was with understanding how linear actuators could be used for the wings. All I'd thought was that they moved in a straight line, but of course that doesn't work for a wing which has to turn around a joint. Studying mechanical linkages and pivot points took a long time, since it took me a while to understand how these motions worked. Eventually, I worked out how the wing structures could transfer the actuator’s movement into a larger wing motion. As it turns out, the linear actuator doesn't actually make the wing turn. Instead, it moves in a straight line and this motion is then passed on through a series of linked bits and turning joints. These essentially change the straight push and pull of the actuator into the move we see on the outside, the turning of the wing. 

I also read a lot about how wing size and weight have a major effect on the amount of force required because wings act as large levers. The farther the weight extends from the pivot point, the greater the torque required to move and support the wings.

I compared and contrasted the various decisions that other people have made when designing and building their own wings. Some went for the simplest approach, with the fewest number of moving parts, while others used more complicated systems to get a more natural-looking and feeling movement. Looking at the pros and cons of each one helped me figure out what I should and shouldn't do with mine. After all this, I felt a lot more confident about creating my own design. So, with a much clearer idea of what makes something like this work, I felt ready to start working on my own design.

I didn't draw my sketches to scale, but I did try to keep the sizing of the pipes on the frame consistent. In total, these sketches honestly took about 6 hours to make. 

While making these designs on paper, I also thought a lot about the materials I wanted to use for this project. I needed a lightweight but durable frame for it to me comfortable to wear while also being able to properly utilize the forces of the linear actuators. I eventually decided on PVC tubing after some recommendations from others, mainly because it is affordable, easy to cut, lightweight, and readily available. It wasn't the strongest material avaiable, but it made more sense to use them over aluminum pipes since they were lighter and easier to handle.

For the mounting plate, I wanted a material that was more rigid while still remaining lightweight. This was so that it was easy to securely attach the actuators and linkage system without introducing unnecessary movement into the frame. I selected ABS plastic because it provided a stronger and flatter surface for attaching mechanical components while remaining lightweight.

I also spent two hours researching the hardware needed to assemble the mechanism. Most of the projects I saw used bolts instead of permanent fasteners because the joints needed to rotate freely around the pivot points. After researching different sizes and types of hardware, I decided to attach the pipes together using M6 bolts, washers, and lock nuts. The washers help reduce friction between moving components while also distributing pressure across the PVC. Lock nuts were selected instead of standard nuts because the wings will experience repeated movement and vibration, which could cause regular nuts to loosen over time. I may use switch back to standard nuts when I actually get my materials if I need to.

I also made a version of the wings with plumage, so people could see how the wings would look in real life. The mechanical frame was my main priority, but I wanted the final project to have a convincing appearance. ## Electrical Planning (7.5 hours)

<img width="3232" height="1848" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/92bfb86d-0da2-4b9f-8d4e-d7d02e4ee84e" />

After completing the mechanical planning, I started designing the electrical system of the wings. I had almost no previous experience with wiring, electrical components, or motor control systems, so I was very clueless as I started this section. Unlike the mechanical design, where I could somewhat already understand how parts moved, I needed to know how power and signals travelled between different components to make the electrical system. Because of this, I had to spend significant time researching basic electrical concepts before I could create a working wiring diagram. 

It took me about 3 hours to finish this. I first researched examples of motor control diagrams. I also looked into how engineers represented electrical connections. I talked to other people and learned how wiring diagrams were not meant to show just how the wires connected each component of my project. These diagrams needed to show the electrical relationships between the components, and wires were not sufficient because I needed to show the difference between the positive and negative connections in my project.

The electrical system consists of a 12V rechargeable battery, a wireless two-channel motor controller, and two 12V linear actuators. The purpose of the system is to allow both wings to move together while being controlled remotely. Since the wings need symmetrical movement, both actuators had to recieve the same control signals at the same time.

The electrical system was designed do exactly that. A linear actuator contains a DC motor, meaning its direction changes depending on the polarity of the electricity supplied. When the positive and negative connections are supplied in one direction, the actuator extends. When the polarity is reversed, the motor spins in the opposite direction, causing the actuator to retract. The motor controller makes the polarity switch automatically, which allows the remote control to operate the wings.

I began planning how all of the components would connect together. I figured the battery would supply power to the motor controller, and the controller would then power both linear actuators. Since the actuators would move together, they'd be connected in parallel rather than in series. Connecting them in parallel allows for both of the actuators to receive the same voltage and operate independently while still responding to the same control signals.

While figuring out the wiring diagram, I also had to figure out how I'd go about wiring the whole thing. I have zero experience with wiring, so I had to talk to a lot of people and do a lot of research on how to wire stuff. I had to learn about different wire types, terminals, connectors, and methods of joining wires. I researched how screw terminals are used to create secure connections between power sources and controllers, and how soldering can be used to permanently join wires between components.

I figured out that I'm going to need to use cables to connect the remote control to the battery and the linear actuators. I'm going to have to connect wires through a screw terminal to connect the battery and the remote control, and I'm going to have to solder wires together to connect the linear actuators and the remote control. This honestly took me about 4.5 hours to figure out because I was starting from scratch with no knowledge about this topic.

## Final Design Summary + Additional Concepts (3.5 hours)

I had finally finished my whole design concept, which consisted of a wearable backpack-mounted animatronic wing system powered by two 12V linear actuators. The mechanical system used PVC tubing, ABS mounting components, and linkage mechanisms to create controlled wing movement. The electrical system used a rechargeable 12V battery, wireless motor controller, and actuator system to provide remote operation. 

Instead of using a simple hinge that would only allow the wings to move upward and downward, the linkage mechanism allowed the wings to fold, extend, and create a more natural movement pattern. The movement of the wings would be controlled using two 12V linear actuators mounted to the central structure. These actuators would convert electrical energy into mechanical movement by extending and retracting, with the linkage system translating this linear motion into the rotational movement needed for the wings. Both actuators would operate together to ensure that the left and right wings moved symmetrically.

I made a few detailed sketches online to truly showcase the final design. These sketches focused on showing how the mechanical frame, actuators, and outer appearance of the wings would come together in a cleaner way than my paper ones. It took me about 3 hours to do, which sounds like a lot but considering I had to carefully recreate my design on a laptop using a mouse, I would say it is pretty believable.
